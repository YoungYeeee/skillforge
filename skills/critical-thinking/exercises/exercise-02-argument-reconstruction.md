# Exercise 02: Argument Reconstruction

**Difficulty:** Intermediate
**Time:** 30–45 minutes
**AI assistance:** Allowed for feedback only — complete the worksheet first

---

## Why This Exercise

Identifying assumptions is step one. This exercise goes further: you take an argument you *disagree with*, reconstruct it in its strongest possible form, and then — and only then — critique it.

This is the difference between someone who argues well and someone who thinks well.

---

## Instructions

**Do the worksheet entirely without AI. Use AI only at the end for feedback.**

**Step 1:** Choose a position you genuinely disagree with. It should be something real — a policy, a common belief, an argument you've heard recently. Avoid topics you feel nothing about.

**Step 2:** State the position in its weakest form first — the version you instinctively reach for.

**Step 3:** Complete the worksheet below to reconstruct it in its strongest form.

**Step 4:** Write a critique of the steelmanned version (not the weak version).

**Step 5:** Use the feedback prompt to evaluate your work.

## Feedback Prompt (Use After Completing the Worksheet)

```
I've completed an argument reconstruction exercise. Please evaluate my work honestly.

The position I chose: [your position]

My steelmanned version: [paste your 3–5 sentence steelman]

My critique: [paste your critique]

Please:
1. Rate the steelman: Does it represent the position fairly and at its strongest? (weak / acceptable / strong)
2. Tell me specifically what would make the steelman stronger
3. Rate the critique: Does it engage with the steelmanned version, or does it attack a weaker version? (strawman / partial / genuine)
4. Identify one assumption in my critique that I haven't examined
5. Overall: what should I practice more?
```

---

## What "Good" Looks Like

A strong steelman will:
- Use the best evidence the other side actually cites, not weak examples you invented
- Reflect the values and concerns that motivate real proponents of this view
- Make someone who holds this view say "that's actually a better version than I usually hear"

A strong critique will:
- Attack the steelmanned version, not the original weak version
- Identify the most important assumption, not just an easy target
- Acknowledge what the argument gets right before explaining what it gets wrong

---

## Do This Exercise Again When

You can steelman a position in real time during a conversation — without writing it down first — and the person who holds that view agrees it's a fair representation. At that point, you're ready for the graduation assessment.
